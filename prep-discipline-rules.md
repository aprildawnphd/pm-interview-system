# Prep Discipline Rules

This is the working methodology behind the prep system in this repo. These rules emerged from running real interview loops, catching real failure modes, and tightening the discipline each time something almost cost me a round. I'm publishing them as rules rather than as a tidy retrospective because that's how they actually function: every prep doc, every question bank update, every pre-call review runs against this list. If you're a senior PM running an AI-augmented prep practice of your own, treat these as starting constraints, not commandments. They are mine. Yours will diverge.

## 1. Predicted-question answers are written out in full, every time

**Rule.** Every predicted question in a prep doc gets a full, deployable, verbatim answer in a blockquote. No pointer references. No "pull from the Brand Brief." No "see the Concerns section."

**Why.** Pointer references work in calm session-prep mode and break in the 2-minute pre-call review. I learned this the hard way before a recruiter screen, sitting with a prep doc that told me where the answer lived instead of giving me the answer. Context-switching across documents 2 minutes before a call is a real failure mode. The prep doc has to be self-contained.

**How to apply.** Compose from the source library (Question Bank, Brand Brief), but copy the relevant text into the predicted-question slot, tune it to the specific role, and leave it ready to read out loud. Italic watch-out notes underneath (length, variant framing, what to avoid) are fine. The answer itself stays full.

## 2. Leadership content is always explicit, never implicit

**Rule.** Any prep doc that touches leadership, team development, culture, accountability, pressure response, trust, or expert-domain partnership gets an explicit Leadership section deployed from the Leadership Brand Brief.

**Why.** Leadership is a near-universal interview topic, and my brand on it (clarity and exposure, honest feedback, high-accountability low-drama, amplify-don't-redesign, player-coach, calmness contagious) is one of my sharpest differentiators. For too many rounds it was getting buried inside generic predicted-question entries and not surfacing as differentiation. Implicit leadership content costs the round.

**How to apply.** At session start, scan the Brand Brief alongside the Question Bank. In every prep doc, include identity statement, 1 or 2 relevant principles, 1 signature line, and 1 proof story. Treat leadership and team development as braided, not separate.

## 3. Question bank is evergreen, scanned at open, updated at close

**Rule.** The master Question Bank is the single reusable prep resource. Scan it at the start of every prep session. Update it at session close.

**Why.** I built the bank to stop re-deriving the same answers in every company-specific prep. If it isn't scanned at open, the prep doc reinvents wheels. If it isn't updated at close, the next loop loses the refinement I just earned.

**How to apply.** Read the bank first. Pull from it for any interview that touches similar topics. Blend across entries when a specific JD needs a fitted response, the bank is a source library, not a script. Fold new Q&A back in at close and regenerate the companion .docx.

## 4. Predicted questions first, then answers in matching order

**Rule.** Structure every company-specific prep guide so the top predicted questions are listed first as a clean ordered list, followed by the answers in the same order. Question N's answer is the Nth entry in the answers section.

**Why.** I want to scan the full question set first, get oriented to what's coming, then read the answers in matching sequence. The older interleaved layout (TMAY in one place, Why-X in another, gap counters elsewhere) made the doc harder to use under pressure.

**How to apply.** Apply to every new guide. Reorganize older guides into this structure when they're next revisited. The Question Bank itself follows a different structure, theme-organized with cross-references, because it serves a different job.

## 5. Deliverable formatting is non-negotiable

**Rule.** No em dashes. No en dashes. Use the % symbol, never the word "percent" (spell it out only if a percentage starts a sentence). Numbers under 10 are numerals, not words: "4 levers," not "four levers."

**Why.** Executive-register written deliverables. The rules are small individually and cumulative in effect, they signal the same care I expect a prep doc to model back at me when I'm reading it under stress.

**How to apply.** Apply to every prep doc and every companion artifact. Regenerate the .docx after edits. Keep a Writing Rule callout near the top of long-running prep docs so the rule is visible to anyone (including future me) editing them.

## 6. Public-facing writing is first-person, not recap

**Rule.** When writing anything public-facing off source material (podcast, article, book), write from my position. My observation, my POV, my stake. Cite the source once, own the rest.

**Why.** Public writing that reads like a third-party recap ("Here's what stuck with me about what X said") leaks authority. The prep practice is itself a public artifact now, and the methodology document, the LinkedIn post, the repo README all need to land in my voice.

**How to apply.** Decide the layer first (counter-argument, personal observation, extension, tension), then draft. Confident first-person throughout.

## 7. Don't pathologize a missing scripted close

**Rule.** The absence of a scripted advance-the-process close ("I would like to move forward, what are the next steps?") is not a deficiency. I close by reading the room.

**Why.** A coaching loop once flagged a "missed-close pattern" as my top behavioral risk across an interview round. The record contradicted it completely: I advanced from every stage of that loop, and interviewer attunement is my most documented differentiator. The coaching was wrong. Scripted closes are 1 tool among several, not a mandate.

**How to apply.** Treat close language as optional. The one legitimate nuance for a final or decision round: make sure genuine commitment to the company lands somewhere before the conversation ends, because the decision-maker may be weighing whether I truly want the role. The form is my call. Broader principle: when I push back on prep feedback, weight it heavily.

## How these rules show up in practice

The artifacts in this repo are all downstream of these rules. The Question Bank reflects rules 3 and 5: evergreen, theme-organized, formatted to the executive-register conventions. The Leadership Brand Brief exists because of rule 2: leadership content needed a dedicated source of truth so it would stop going implicit. The company-specific prep template enforces rules 1 and 4: full verbatim answers in blockquotes, predicted questions listed first with answers in matching order. The Writing Rule callout in every long-form prep doc enforces rule 5 against my own future drift. And the absence of a mandated close section enforces rule 7: the prep system trusts my judgment in the room.

The rules are the methodology. The artifacts are the rules made operational.
