# Interview Prep Methodology: Standard Operating Guide

**Purpose.** This document explains how I built and ran a senior product leadership interview prep practice across a multi-loop 2026 job search. It covers the workflow, the writing rules, the operating principles, and the verification discipline that keeps drift out of the system across many concurrent tracks.

This is the methodology layer. The artifacts it produces consume this guide.

## Why this exists

Two pressures shaped the project.

**The first is reuse.** Across multiple interview cycles, roughly 60 to 70 percent of the working content is structurally identical: headline credentials, the verified DRIVE storybank, the recruiter screen question bank, the questions-to-ask architecture, the watch-outs, the acronym reference. Re-litigating these every cycle wastes time and risks drift. Holding them in a stable template plus an evergreen question bank protects continuity and frees each new prep session to focus on the 30 percent that is genuinely role-specific.

**The second is consistency.** Early prep documents were built ground-up. They had different section orders, different naming conventions, and slightly different framings of identical concepts. A template enforces a shared structure so any prep document can be navigated the same way, and so a fresh AI session can reliably pick up a prior document and extend it without re-reading the whole thing.

The template is not a script. It is the skeleton on which a tailored prep document gets built.

## How to use the template (the workflow)

### Step 1: Spin up a new prep document

For a new active interview track, copy the template to a new file named `[Company]_[Role]_Interview_Prep.md`. Fill in the header block at the top with the role title, hiring manager, recruiter, and status. Do not touch anything else yet.

### Step 2: Capture the raw inputs

Before drafting any answers, populate the factual sections in this order:

1. **Section 1 (About the Company) and 1B (About the Product).** Pull from public sources: company website, JD, news, funding announcements, executive interviews, recent product launches. Search for the company name plus terms like "ARR," "PE-backed," "acquisition," "AI strategy," and the names of the hiring manager and any board members. If the company is PE-backed or recently acquired, prioritize understanding the capital thesis. This work is foundational; everything later depends on it.

2. **Section 1C (Market and Competitive Landscape).** Map direct competitors, adjacent competitors, and emerging or AI-native threats. Identify the company's defensible position and its honest vulnerabilities. The "why this role exists now" subsection is where the strategic read-between-the-lines work happens; it shapes every answer in Section 5.

3. **Section 2 (About the Role).** Paste in the JD's exact language for primary objectives and must-haves. Paraphrase the nice-to-haves. Then write the "what the role actually does" decoded section that translates the JD into what the work will look like on day 1, day 100, and day 365.

### Step 3: Position against the role

4. **Section 3 (Strengths).** The most important section in the document. Write the headline "Why I am the right fit for this specific role" paragraph first. Everything else in the document hangs off this. Then build the Tier 1 / Tier 2 / Tier 3 stack, and lock in the three-sentence recruiter brief that will get planted at the end of the screen.

5. **Section 4 (Gaps).** Be honest. Every role has at least one gap and the recruiter will probe it. Write the bridge framing carefully so the gap-handling reads neither defensive nor hedging.

### Step 4: Draft the answers

6. **Section 5 (Questions to Be Asked of Me, With Answers).** Draft TMAY first; it is the spine of every other answer. Then draft the role-specific answers in 5K. Then assemble the question banks in 5H and 5I from the Question Bank plus role-specific additions. **Predicted-question answers must be full deployable verbatim text in blockquotes, not pointer references like "see Question Bank" or "Direction: [shorthand].** The candidate should be able to read the prep doc end-to-end in the 2-minute pre-call review without context-switching. See `prep-discipline-rules.md` for the full rule.

7. **Section 6 (Questions to Ask Them).** Customize the top-4 and top-5 lists with the company's product name plugged in. Build the extended bank by adapting prior prep documents.

### Step 5: Build the strategic spine

8. **Section 7 (Strategic Frameworks).** Where senior-level fluency gets demonstrated. The PE thesis (or capital thesis), what the company really sells, the productization pattern, the AI strategy frame, and the strategic vision answer all developed here. The vision answer should mirror the JD's exact vocabulary; that signal alone separates strong candidates from also-rans.

### Step 6: Wrap with reference and operating discipline

9. **Sections 8 through 12** (strategic facts, top messages, watch-outs, one-sentence pitch, acronyms) are reference material to scan the night before and the morning of. Keep them tight. The acronym table earns its place when the category uses unfamiliar terminology.

10. **Sections 13 and 14** (open questions, working notes) are operational. They tell the next prep session where to start. Update them every time the document changes.

## Operating principles for every prep cycle

### Writing rules (firm, every document)

- **No em dashes or en dashes.** Anywhere. Use commas, periods, or restructured phrasing. Dashes increasingly read as AI-generated to recruiters and hiring managers.
- **VP-level tone throughout.** Confident, outcome-oriented, strategic, no filler.
- **Lead with bold value.** Every drafted answer opens with the headline, then supports it with one or two data points. Save the rest for follow-ups.
- **Do not revise approved copy without explicit direction.** Once a TMAY or DRIVE story version is signed off, it stays locked unless explicitly opened for revision.
- **Numbers under 10 as numerals; use % not "percent."** Reads more crisply at executive altitude.
- **Predicted-question answers in full deployable text, never pointers.** See `prep-discipline-rules.md`.

### Content rules

- **Mirror the JD's vocabulary.** When the JD says "0-to-1 commercialization" or "AI pragmatist" or "Copilots and Decision Engines," use those exact phrases back. It signals close reading.
- **Source every metric against a verified portfolio context document.** Maintain a single source of truth for portfolio facts and verified DRIVE story outcomes. If a number is not in the canonical storybank, do not improvise. Either confirm against the source of truth or omit.
- **Lead with commercial credentials.** Do not lead with PhD or scientist narrative unless the audience is scientific, life-sciences, or mission-driven. The PhD is a credibility add inside an answer, not the headline. Audience-specific rule: mission-driven companies (clean energy, sustainability, education, health-equity) get the PhD-and-values framing as authentic mission alignment; commercial SaaS audiences do not.
- **Three credentials, not seven.** Pick the two or three most relevant verified stories for each role and let the rest support. The umbrella meta-story (mature-platform extension via multiple commercial levers) is the most reusable.
- **Leadership content must be explicit, not implicit.** When building any prep doc that touches leadership, team development, culture, accountability, pressure response, trust, or expert-domain partnership, deploy content explicitly from the Leadership Brand Brief. Do not assume the question won't come up.

### Process rules

- **Read the canonical portfolio context and any prior prep documents before starting a new one.** Decisions made in earlier cycles (no PhD lead, no dashes, verified storybank) carry forward by default. Re-litigating wastes time.
- **Update the Working Notes section every session.** A fresh session and a future self both rely on this to pick up where the last session left off.
- **Track verification debt.** The Open Questions section names what is unverified. Resolve before the live interview, not in the prep loop.

### Verification discipline

Earlier prep cycles propagated unverified "locked stories" from one track to the next without confirming against the source of truth. This created a real failure mode: a prep doc carried a "three locked DRIVE stories" framing inherited from prior work, and two of the three were either misframed or had no execution outcome. The error was caught before the live screen, but the fix made verification an explicit discipline:

- **Verify stories against the canonical portfolio context, not against prior prep docs.** Prior prep docs may carry forward errors.
- **A story with no execution outcome is design-thinking, not a closed narrative.** Deploy as "how would you approach this kind of transformation," never as "here is what I delivered."
- **A label may be misframing.** Re-anchor against the actual underlying story before reusing.
- **When in doubt, ask.** Do not improvise or carry framings forward without verification.

### Question-asking rules (Section 6)

- Three to four questions per conversation, not all of them.
- Strongest question first. Recency bias works both ways.
- Listen to the answer before asking the next question. Connecting the next question to what was just said is the highest-signal interview move.
- Save one question for the close, focused on commitment and next steps.

## What carries forward across every role (the stable 70%)

The following content is structurally stable across interview tracks. Each new prep document inherits it from the canonical portfolio context and prior cycles, and tailors only the closing sentence or example.

- **TMAY backbone.** Anchored on the headline operator credentials (portfolio scale, EBITDA, growth-in-contraction), the umbrella growth framework, AI workflow fluency, and the closing bridge sentence (which is the part that changes per role).
- **The verified DRIVE storybank.** A small set of canonical stories with deploy-for guidance, full STAR text, earned secrets, and audience adaptation notes. Maintained in the Question Bank Section 14.
- **The reason-for-leaving framing.** Brief, non-emotional, forward-looking, locked to a single sentence with a forward-looking bridge.
- **The compensation framing.** Aligned-with-band and above-band versions. Default to deflect-first. "Total compensation," never "salary."
- **The questions-to-ask architecture.** Top 4 for recruiter screen, top 5 for hiring manager, plus extended banks per stakeholder type.
- **The questions-to-avoid list.**
- **The writing rules and acronym reference.**
- **The watch-outs.** Standing rules on what not to lead with, what not to deploy, what not to write.

## What changes per role (the role-specific 30%)

The following content is fully role-specific and gets rebuilt each time:

- **Sections 1, 1B, 1C.** Company, product, market context.
- **Section 2.** Role objectives and success measures.
- **Section 3 headline.** The "why me for this specific role" paragraph.
- **Section 4 gaps.** The bridge framing tailored to the specific gap the recruiter will probe.
- **Section 5D variations.** The industry-fit bridge tailored to the specific industry parallel.
- **Section 5K role-specific answers.** "What would you stop doing?", "How will you partner with [counterpart]?", 30/60/90, and any role-specific drafted answers.
- **Section 7 strategic frame.** PE thesis (or capital thesis), productization pattern, AI strategy frame, strategic tensions, strategic vision answer.
- **Top messages in Section 9.** Calibrated to the five themes that matter most for this specific role.

## How to start a new prep session with an AI assistant

The methodology was built and run with an AI coach (Claude Code). The workflow scales to any capable AI assistant that can read prior documents and extend them.

1. **Tell the assistant which company and role this is for.** If a `[Company]_[Role]_Interview_Prep.md` file already exists, the assistant reads it first before generating anything new.

2. **If no prep document exists yet, the assistant copies `Interview_Prep_Template.md`** and creates the new working file, then asks for the JD, the recruiter notes, and the names of the known stakeholders.

3. **The assistant proceeds through the workflow above in order:** company context first, then role decoding, then positioning, then drafted answers, then strategic spine, then reference and operating discipline. Each session ends by updating Sections 13 and 14 with what was decided and what the next session should open with.

4. **Push back when you disagree.** Approved copy stays locked unless explicitly opened for revision.

5. **Every drafted answer is delivered with the metadata block:** when to use, target length, full text, why it works, watch-outs.

## What I built around early gaps

Running this system across many tracks surfaced gaps in the original SOP. The gaps below were operationalized inside the methodology over time. The artifacts that closed them are now part of this repo.

- **Verification checklist.** Operationalized via the canonical portfolio context as the single source of truth for portfolio facts and the verified storybank. Every metric verifiable against a single document.
- **Leadership philosophy artifact.** Operationalized via `Leadership_Brand_Brief.md`. A standalone artifact that can be referenced in TMAY, scanned at session start alongside the Question Bank, and consumed by any prep doc that touches leadership content.
- **Reusable component library.** Operationalized via `Interview_Question_Bank.md`, the cross-company evergreen library of canonical answers with audience variants.
- **Post-round debrief discipline.** Operationalized via a dedicated `Round Outcome` section at the bottom of every prep doc, captured same-day after the interview, scored on a 5-dimension rubric (Substance, Structure, Relevance, Credibility, Differentiation), and reconciled against the candidate's at-the-round self-read for calibration.
- **Competing-loops tracker.** Operationalized via a single `INDEX.md` at the active-projects root, tracking every active track, current round, status, and next action across the full search.

## Maintenance

This methodology gets reviewed every 90 days, or after every two completed interview cycles. The review checks:

- Whether the template has drifted from how prep documents are actually being built
- Whether the verified storybank is still the strongest fit or whether candidate stories should be promoted to verified status
- Whether new categories of role need their own template variants
- Whether new discipline rules have emerged that should be added to `prep-discipline-rules.md`

The system is mine; AI assistants can be asked to audit prior prep documents against the template and the portfolio context doc and surface drift.
