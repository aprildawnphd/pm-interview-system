## 5H. Most Likely Recruiter Screen Questions

The recruiter screen is 20 to 30 minutes. The recruiter is filtering for fit, not testing strategic chops. Questions cluster around: who you are, why you, why now, can the hiring manager use the brief, and the logistics that would kill a hire.

**Background and fit**
- Tell me about yourself.
- Walk me through your career trajectory.
- Describe your most recent role and the scope of what you owned.
- Why did you leave your previous role?
- What size portfolio did you manage and how was P&L structured?
- How many direct reports? Indirect reports? What levels?

**Why this role, why now**
- What about this role caught your attention?
- What do you know about the company and product so far?
- Have you worked in [domain] before?
- What kind of role are you looking for next?
- How does this role fit with your career goals?

**Capability probes (lighter than hiring manager)**
- Tell me about a time you [primary objective from JD].
- How have you used AI in your products?
- Tell me about a time you led [adjacent objective from JD].
- What's your experience with [nice-to-have from JD]?
- How do you develop product managers on your team?

**Logistics that can kill a hire**
- Compensation expectations?
- Location and remote fit?
- Travel comfort?
- Start date?
- Work authorization?
- Actively interviewing elsewhere?
- Anything required to onboard or relocate?

**Closing**
- Do you have questions for me? *(See Section 6.)*
- Anything you want me to share with the hiring manager? *(The gift question — plant the three-sentence brief from Section 3.)*

## 5I. Most Likely Hiring Manager Round Questions

The hiring manager round is 45 to 60 minutes and substantively different. Group by what each question is testing.

**Strategy and product judgment**
- Walk me through your strategic vision for [the product].
- Where would you focus product investment in the first 12 months?
- How would you think about prioritization across [the JD's named tensions]?
- How would you defend the product's positioning against [named competitor]?
- How do you think about AI's threat and opportunity in [the category]?
- How would you think about build vs. buy vs. partner for AI capabilities?
- What would you stop doing?

**Commercial execution**
- Walk me through a commercial outcome you drove.
- Tell me about a time you improved a self-service funnel.
- How do you think about pricing and packaging for [this kind of platform]?
- What metrics would you watch in the first 90 days?
- How would you measure success at 6 / 12 months?
- Talk to me about retention. How have you driven NRR improvement?

**Operating excellence**
- What does product operating discipline mean to you?
- How do you think about prioritization frameworks?
- How do you handle the tension between roadmap commitments and emerging opportunities?
- Walk me through how you'd run a quarterly planning cycle.
- How do you make trade-offs between speed and quality?
- Tell me about a time you killed a project or feature.

**Leadership and people**
- How do you develop PMs?
- Tell me about a PM you hired or promoted who became a strong leader.
- What's your management style?
- How do you give difficult feedback?
- How do you handle a low performer?
- Describe a time you had to influence without authority.

**Behavioral signal questions**
- Tell me about a time you [headline behavioral prompt aligned to this role].
- Describe the most complex [X] you've led.
- Describe a time you disagreed with executive leadership.
- What's a product decision you got wrong, and what did you learn?

**Industry-specific probes**
- What's changing fastest in [this category]?
- What's the most interesting AI use case in [this category] you've seen?
- Do you have a point of view on [emerging trend]?

**The "why us, why now" close**
- Why this role over your other options?
- What concerns do you have about this role?
- What would have to be true for you to take this offer?
- What's your timeline for making a decision?

## 6. Questions to Ask Them

> Two operating principles: (1) Audience match — ask each interviewer what only they can answer well. (2) Frame planting — every question signals what you care about. Choose questions that reinforce the frame you want left in the room after you leave.

### Operating rules for delivery

- **Pick three to four per conversation, not all of them.** Asking ten signals a list. Asking three signals genuine curiosity.
- **Ask the strongest question first.** Recency bias works both ways; the opener is what they remember.
- **Listen to the answer before asking the next question.** Connecting the next question to what they just said is the highest-signal interview move.
- **Save one question for the close.** When they ask "any final questions?" have one ready that brings the conversation back to commitment and next steps.

### 6A. Questions for the Recruiter Screen

**Top 4 to actually use:**

1. **"What did the hiring manager describe as their top priority for this hire, and what would make someone an obvious yes for them?"**
2. **"What types of profiles is the hiring manager seeing in the candidate pool, and where does mine sit relative to those?"**
3. **"Walk me through the interview process from here. Who would I meet, in what sequence, and what's each interviewer focused on?"**
4. **"Is there anything in my background you anticipate the hiring manager will want to dig into, or anything you'd like me to clarify or send before the next round?"**

**Extended bank:**

[On the role and the business, on the hiring manager, on the candidate pool and process, on the candidate's positioning, on comp and logistics, and the close. Adapt from your established prep doc patterns.]

### 6B. Questions for the Hiring Manager Round

**Top 5 to actually use:**

1. **"What does [the product] need to do over the next 12 to 18 months that the current product organization isn't yet set up to do?"**
2. **"How would you describe [the product]'s commercial moat today, and where do you see it being most at risk over the next 24 months?"**
3. **"What's the relationship between [the product] and the broader [parent / ecosystem] from a product standpoint? Where does that integration create the most value, and where does it create the most friction?"**
4. **"What's the most important thing the existing product team needs from this hire? And what's the thing they most fear?"**
5. **"If we're sitting here in 12 months and this hire has worked, what's different about the business? And what's different about the product organization?"**

**Extended bank:**

[On product strategy and the platform, on AI specifically, on commercial execution, on the team and operating model, on the hiring manager and reporting relationship, on the parent ecosystem, on the role itself, and the close.]

### 6C. Questions for Other Stakeholders (Engineering, Sales, CS, Peer Leaders, Board)

[Tailored question banks for each stakeholder type the candidate will meet later in the process.]

### 6D. Questions to Avoid

- "Tell me about the company culture." (Too generic at this level.)
- "What's the work-life balance like?" (Reads defensive at Senior Director and above.)
- "What does a typical day look like?" (Senior leaders don't have typical days.)
- "What's the tech stack?" (Wrong audience for a product leader.)
- "Are you happy with the current product?" (Puts the interviewer on the spot uncomfortably.)
- Any question whose answer is on the company website or in the JD.

### 6E. The Hidden Question to Ask Yourself

One question to keep running silently in the background of every interview conversation: **"Would I want this job if it were offered to me today, on the terms I'm being shown?"** If yes, push for the offer. If no, the interview becomes practice and a relationship-building exercise, not a decision-forcing event. The candidate genuinely willing to walk away tends to land the strongest offer.

## 10. Watch-Outs and Operating Principles

### General interview watch-outs

- Don't pitch a reorganization unless the JD explicitly invites it. Most existing teams are high-caliber; amplify, don't redesign.
- Don't over-promise the 30/60/90. Listening first is respected.

- Don't get pulled into feature debates. Redirect to commercial logic.
- Don't minimize structural complexities the JD names (dual-channel, multi-segment, internal-vs-external).
- Don't lead with science background or PhD unless the audience is scientific. Stay on commercial credentials.
- Don't use em or en dashes in any deliverables.
